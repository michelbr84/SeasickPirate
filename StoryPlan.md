Story Plan — The Pirate Who Was Afraid of the Sea
Author: Marin Bloom (assigned; change if you prefer)
Title: The Pirate Who Was Afraid of the Sea
Subtitle: A Tall Tale of Small Bravery
Target Age Range: 6–8 years — early reader / picture book (Guided Reading Level: K–M; Grades 1–3)
Genre & Format: Humorous early-reader / picture book. Estimated length: ~32 pages; manuscript approx. 850–950 words. Picture-book spread layout with repeated lines and rhythmic phrases for read‑aloud.

Core Theme and Central Question
- Core theme: Overcoming fear through many small, brave actions and humor.
- Central question: Can Captain Pip learn to face the sea—one tiny brave step at a time—without losing his laugh?

Main Message / Moral
- True courage is not the absence of fear but choosing to try, laugh, and keep going despite it.

Short Synopsis
- Captain Pip Finn loves maps and songs but gets seasick whenever the ship wobbles. When a crinkled map to Whispering Isle appears, Pip must lead his quirky crew to treasure. With Lula’s playful practices, Old Sal’s breathing trick, and the crew’s cheering, Pip learns to take many small braves—stand at the wheel for a minute, sing a line, breathe slowly—so that by the time Whispering Isle appears he can sail on with a grin, still a little queasy but braver than before.

Main Characters (for editors & illustrators)
- Captain Pip Finn — Main protagonist. Small, earnest, big hat, big heart. Loves treasure maps and pirate songs. Gets seasick when the ship rocks. Inventive and gentle.
- First Mate Lula — Ally and supportive friend. Parrot-hat-wearing, brave, practical, playful. Teases Pip with affectionate jokes and creates games to help him practice.
- Old Sal — Mentor figure. Retired mariner, storyteller, keeps helpful breathing trick and tall tales in his sea chest. Warm and calming presence.
- The Crew (Finn’s Shipmates) — Ensemble. Singing cook, nervous cabin boy, dancing deckhand, others: comic relief, varied emotional mirrors and creative supporters.
- Mischief the Wave — Personified minor antagonist. Playful, splashy wave that pops up to test Pip’s nerve but is never cruel—more like a cheeky friend.

Setting & Atmosphere
- Timeless, slightly cartoonish pirate ship on a warm, friendly sea. Islands wink on the horizon. Visual palette: warm aquas, sunny golds, coral pinks; textures: crinkled map paper, splashes of white foam, sunflecks on wood. Mood blends humor with gentle tension.

Emotional & Educational Goals
- Normalize fearful feelings and show strategies to manage them in small steps.
- Teach simple coping tools (Old Sal’s breathing technique).
- Encourage empathy—how a team can adapt to support someone.
- Promote humor as a reframe to reduce stress.
- Support early readers with repetitive phrases, short sentences, and visual cues.

Differentiation & Selling Points
- Humor-driven reversal of pirate tropes (brave captain who’s afraid of the sea).
- Practical emotional tools embedded for classroom/home use.
- Personified wave (Mischief) gives playful read-aloud interactions.
- Ensemble cast provides variety in both illustration and reading rhythm.

Suggested Page Breakdown & Scene Beats (approximate 32 pages)
- Pages 1–2 (Title spread): Title, subtitle, author.
- Pages 3–4 (Intro): Introduce Pip, big hat, love of maps and songs; quick gag about seasickness.
- Pages 5–6 (Inciting map): Finding the crinkled map to Whispering Isle; Pip’s excitement then worry.
- Pages 7–10 (Decision & departure): Pip decides to sail; crew prepares; initial rocking makes Pip queasy.
- Pages 11–14 (Training & practice): Lula’s games (balance, silly shanties), Old Sal’s breathing trick revealed, crew’s supportive antics.
- Pages 15–18 (Mischief appears): First encounter with Mischief the Wave; Pip tries small brave acts, sometimes laughs, sometimes leans on crew.
- Pages 19–22 (Progress & setbacks): Pip grows braver through repeated tiny acts; a big wobble makes him doubt but he uses breathing and team support.
- Pages 23–26 (Final test): Near Whispering Isle, Mischief returns for a final splash; Pip uses his new tricks and leads briefly.
- Pages 27–30 (Treasure & payoff): Landing at Whispering Isle, treasure chest opens to find silly souvenirs and a mirror of friendship/courage.
- Pages 31–32 (End pages / back matter): Closing moral with quick breathing exercise ("Old Sal’s Secret"), Lula’s mini-games, author note placeholder.

Title: The Pirate Who Was Afraid of the Sea

--- Page Break ---
Captain Pip Finn had a hat so big the gulls used it for cloud-watching.

He loved treasure maps and pirate songs and peppermints for the road. But whenever the ship gave a wobble—WHOOP!—Pip’s stomach did a flip, his knees went jelly, and he turned as green as a sea cucumber.

[Illustration note: Full-bleed spread of Pip on deck, oversized hat, bright sea, gulls perched on hat. Show Pip clutching his stomach. Warm palette.]

--- Page Break ---
“That’s just the sea saying hello!” First Mate Lula squawked, tugging a tiny parrot-feather from her hat. Lula loved a good wobble. She liked tying rope in tricky knots and dancing on the railing.

Pip laughed, then promptly turned three shades paler.

[Illustration note: Lula playful, tugging feather from parrot-hat; Pip smiling weakly. Crew in background: singing cook, nervous cabin boy.] 

--- Page Break ---
One rainy morning the ship’s bell rang and the deck creaked like an old song. Someone spotted a crinkled map fluttering against the mast.

“Whispering Isle!” the cook sang. “Treasure! Treasure!” The cabin boy squeaked. The map smelled of salt and secrets.

Pip’s eyes sparkled. “We must go,” he said, but his voice shook like a sail in wind.

[Illustration note: Map close-up, crinkled with dotted line to Whispering Isle. Pip’s eager face, then worried.]

--- Page Break ---
So they set sail. The crew hummed a shanty. Pip took the wheel. The ship swayed. WHOOP! Pip’s stomach did a somersault.

“I—” he said. He swallowed the words and wished for land.

Lula hopped up beside him. “One minute at the wheel,” she said. “I’ll time you. Sing one line. Then you can sit.”

Pip nodded. He stood, sang a single brave line (off-key but loud), and sat down with a wobble. The crew clapped. Pip giggled despite the queasy flip.

[Illustration note: Sequence panels: Pip at wheel, singing a line; Lula with hand up counting; crew clapping; Pip giggling but pale.]

--- Page Break ---
In the quarterdeck shadow sat Old Sal, whose beard hid tiny seashells. He told stories about waves that told jokes and winds that hummed lullabies.

“Try the breathing trick,” Old Sal murmured, handing Pip a peppermint. “Breathe in like you’re smelling the sweetest sea-apple. Hold it. Blow it out like a whisper to the waves.”

Pip tried. Breathe in. Hold. Whisper out. The quease loosened like a knot untying.

[Illustration note: Old Sal leaning toward Pip, soft lighting, peppermint between fingers; breathing steps shown with simple arrows and text.]

--- Page Break ---
Mischief the Wave loved a good splash. Mischief popped up suddenly, puffing salty breath like a giggling child. WHOOSH! A spray tickled the deck.

Pip stiffened. Lula sang a silly line and did a quick balancing trick—hop left, hop right—so Pip copied and wobbled with a laugh.

“Tiny brave!” Lula cheered. “One step, then another.”

Pip took another minute at the wheel. He breathed with Old Sal and sang with Lula. He laughed when his hat slid over his eyes.

[Illustration note: Mischief as a smiling, expressive wave; series of small actions: Pip balancing, breathing, laughing as hat slips.]

--- Page Break ---
Days drifted by like floating leaves. Some days Pip stood at the wheel for a minute and then sat. Some days he could only peek over the rail. Each time, the crew found a new silly trick.

The cook belted a rhyme that made Pip giggle. The cabin boy told a not-scary ghost joke. The deckhand twirled, then steadied Pip with a hand on his elbow.

Pip learned that brave did not mean not feeling wobbly. It meant standing up, trying, and choosing to laugh when the sea jigged his knees.

[Illustration note: Montage of crew helping with different tricks; warm, supportive expressions.]

--- Page Break ---
Near Whispering Isle Mischief came back for one big splash. WHOOPWHOOPWHOOP! The ship shivered. Pip felt the old green churn.

For a heartbeat he wanted to run below. Then he remembered Old Sal’s breath, Lula’s tiny brave, the cook’s silly rhyme, the cabin boy’s joke. He took a peppermint, breathed in, counted to three, and hummed the shanty with the crew.

Pip stood at the wheel longer than he’d ever stood before. He steered. He laughed. His hat tipped and he caught it without dropping the map.

[Illustration note: Climactic spread: big wave, Pip at wheel with hat mid-air, crew cheering; bold, dynamic composition.]

--- Page Break ---
When the ship nudged Whispering Isle’s sand, the treasure chest didn’t hold gold coins or crowns. It held a small mirror, a packet of peppermints, and a ribbon that read: “For small braves.”

Pip held the mirror and saw his brave face—still a little pale, but smiling.

“You did it, Captain,” Lula said, tucking a feather into his hat. Old Sal winked. The crew danced. Mischief gave one last playful splash and tiptoed away, already planning tomorrow’s prank.

[Illustration note: Treasure spread: silly treasures, mirror reflecting Pip’s smile; celebration.]

--- Page Break ---
Back on deck, Pip kept his big hat and his peppermint jar. He still felt wobbles now and then. But when the ship rolled, he’d breathe like Old Sal, do a little Lula-hop, or tell the crew a silly line.

Bravery, Pip learned, wasn’t a single roar. It was a pocketful of tiny trys.

[Illustration note: Cozy end spread: Pip at wheel, peppermint jar, crew close, Mischief on horizon as friendly wave.]

Back Matter (one page)
Old Sal’s Secret (Breathing Exercise)
1. Breathe in like you smell a sea-apple. Count to three.
2. Hold it like a secret.
3. Blow out and whisper to the waves.

Lula’s Brave Games (3 quick ideas)
- One-Minute Wheel: Stand at the wheel for one minute; clap when you finish.
- Silly Shanty: Sing one silly line when you feel wobbly.
- Hat Catch: Drop a soft hat and catch it—practice focus.

Discussion Prompts
- What is Pip afraid of? Have you ever felt like Pip?
- What small brave could you try this week?
